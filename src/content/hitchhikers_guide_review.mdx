> Don't Panic.

# How I Got Here

I was seventeen, sitting in the back of a car on a six-hour road trip to Jaipur, desperately trying to find something to read that wasn't a textbook. My cousin had this battered paperback wedged between the seat and the door — spine cracked, cover half torn, the kind of book that's clearly been read by five different people and left in five different places. He handed it to me without saying anything, just gave me this look like *you'll figure it out.*

That book was *The Hitchhiker's Guide to the Galaxy* by Douglas Adams.

I finished it before we crossed the Rajasthan border. Then I sat quietly for the rest of the drive, staring out the window, trying to process what I'd just read.

It starts with Earth being demolished to make way for a hyperspace bypass.

</Block>

<Block>
# The Setup

Arthur Dent wakes up one Thursday morning to find a bulldozer outside his house. The local council wants to knock it down for a road. He lies down in the mud in protest. While he's doing that, his friend Ford Prefect — who Arthur has always assumed is a slightly odd out-of-work actor from Guildford — tells him, very calmly, that he is in fact an alien from a planet near Betelgeuse, and that the Earth is about to be destroyed in approximately twelve minutes.

From that point forward, Arthur Dent does not have a good time.

What makes the book work — what makes it *extraordinary* — is that Douglas Adams plays all of this completely straight. Arthur is not a hero. He's a man in a dressing gown who really wanted a cup of tea. The universe does not care. It proceeds to destroy his planet, strand him in space, and introduce him to a series of situations that make less and less sense, all described in prose so precise and deadpan that the absurdity becomes genuinely funny rather than tiresome.

There's a particular paragraph near the beginning where Adams describes how the Vogons — the alien bureaucrats who demolish Earth — are not actually evil in the classic sense. He writes that it is not that they are cruel, it is just that they are thoroughly indifferent to whether you are alive or not, and that they have elevated this indifference into a governing philosophy. I read that in a car going 120 km/h through the Rajasthan desert and had to read it again because it felt like something I had been trying to articulate for years about a certain kind of institutional behaviour I'd encountered and never had words for.

That happens constantly in this book. Adams will say something in three sentences that would take most writers an essay.

</Block>

<Block>
# What It's Actually About

People describe this as a comedy. It is. But that framing undersells it.

Adams is doing something precise underneath the jokes. The book is, at its core, about meaninglessness — and about how you carry on anyway. The central gag, the one that's become so famous it's almost a cliché now, is that the answer to the ultimate question of life, the universe, and everything is 42. A supercomputer called Deep Thought spends seven and a half million years computing this answer, and when it finally announces it, no one knows what to do with it because nobody ever really knew what the question was.

When I was seventeen I thought this was just a funny joke about existentialism.

The older I get, the more I think Adams actually believed it. That we construct elaborate frameworks — religions, philosophies, productivity systems, career plans — to give ourselves the sense that there's a question we're answering. And the unsettling truth might be that there isn't one. Or there is, but we'll never be coherent enough, or alive long enough, to know it.

He doesn't say this grimly. He says it with a kind of cosmic shrug. *Don't panic.* Keep moving. Have a Pan Galactic Gargle Blaster if you can find one.

</Block>

<Block>
# The Writing Itself

Adams's prose is the kind you stop and reread not because it's unclear but because it's too good to move past quickly.

There's a description of the Vogon poetry reading that is one of the funniest paragraphs in the English language. There's a moment where Adams describes what it feels like to be struck by a Vogon's recitation as analogous to being hit repeatedly over the head with a slice of lemon wrapped around a large gold brick — and the precision of that image, the specificity of it, is what makes it work. Not *very painful.* Not *excruciating.* That exact, specific, absurd thing.

He also has a gift for the perfectly placed anticlimax. Sentences that build to something and then land somewhere unexpected. You start to trust his rhythm, and then he uses that trust against you, and it's always funnier for it.

I've tried to describe his style to people before and it's difficult. It's not *wacky.* It's not random. Everything is purposeful, even the things that seem structurally insane. The planet Magrathea — which builds other planets to order for very rich people — is not a throwaway joke. It's a fully developed idea that Adams commits to entirely, and that commitment is what makes it land.

</Block>

<Block>
# A Note on Rereading It

I've read this book four times now. Once in that car at seventeen, once in college when I was going through something I don't need to explain here, once last year when I found my own copy in a second-hand shop in Paharganj, and once in the last few days while writing this.

Each time it's a different book. Not because it changes, but because I do.

The seventeen-year-old me found it hilarious and a little overwhelming. The college-me found it weirdly comforting — there's something about a universe that doesn't care being framed as *funny* rather than *terrifying* that helps when you're not doing well. The more recent me keeps stopping at the philosophical scaffolding Adams builds so casually, marvelling at how much he fits into a book that's only 224 pages.

It is a short book. You can read it in an afternoon. I would recommend you do.

</Block>

# The Parts That Stuck

A few specific things I keep thinking about, years later:

**The Babel fish.** Adams invents a small yellow fish that you put in your ear and it translates any language in the universe for you. He then uses it as the basis for a proof of God's non-existence, which God's non-existence promptly causes, which means by contrapositive that the Babel fish has caused more wars than anything else in history. This takes about half a page and is one of the most compact pieces of logical comedy I've ever read.

**Marvin.** The Paranoid Android. A robot with a brain the size of a planet who is asked to open doors and park spaceships. His depression is not played for cheap laughs — it's played for *accurate* laughs. Anyone who has ever been significantly overqualified for something they were doing will recognise Marvin with uncomfortable specificity.

**The whale.** There is a scene where a sperm whale is suddenly brought into existence miles above the planet Magrathea, falling. The book follows its thoughts in the few seconds before it hits the ground. It has never existed before. It is experiencing everything for the first time. It wonders what this strange ground-rushing-up thing is and whether they will be friends.

I'm not going to say more about that scene. You should read it fresh.

</Block>

<Block>
# Should You Read It

Yes. Obviously yes.

If you like science fiction: yes. If you don't like science fiction but you like books that make you think: yes. If you're having a rough go of it and want something that will make the universe feel slightly less hostile: yes.

It's not a perfect book. The plot is deliberately loose — Adams is more interested in ideas than narrative momentum, and if you need your stories to go somewhere specific you might find it frustrating. The ending is abrupt in the way that suggests Adams was making it up as he went, which he largely was. There are sequels, four of them plus a sixth one written by Eoin Colfer after Adams died, and their quality varies. The first book stands alone.

I've recommended this book to maybe a dozen people over the years. I don't think anyone has come back disappointed. The ones who got it really got it. My cousin who handed me that copy in the back seat of a car — I texted him a few years later to tell him what that book had meant to me. He replied with two words.

*Don't panic.*
